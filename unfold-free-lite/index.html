About Melanoma

Melanoma is one out of a triad of skin cancers, and it is also the most severe. Care while it is in remission is the most important step to take to lead the life you want now and in the future.

The Facts

Melanoma occurs when cell growth is uncontrolled in the melanin-producing cells of your body. This loss of regulation can be due to many causes, most typically UV exposure and genetics. While the latter is uncontrollable, you can take control of your health now and limit your exposure to the sun's harmful UV rays. There are two other malignant, but not as severe types of skin cancer, Squamous Cell Carcinoma, and Basal Cell Carcinoma. Differentiating between these three categories, as well as benign lesions, is an essential step in your treatment.

Causes and How To Avoid Them

Melanoma is a tricky disease, and it can strike at any moment. Learn how to fight back.

UV Radiation

The sun's rays can penetrate all the way to the bottom of the lower layer of your skin, damaging DNA and proteins.

Oncogenes

Certain populations have a single or multiple faulty regulation genes that promote cell division, resulting in programmed proliferation of cells. Can be inherited or acquired by UV exposure.

Tumor Supressor Genes

While the genes that promote cell growth may be normal (wild type), other genes that restrict cell growth may become faulty. Also may be inherited or acquired by UV exposure.

Protect Your Skin

Avoid tanning beds, wear clothes that covers as much skin as possible, and wear high strength suncreen when avoiding the sun is not an option.

Immunotherapy

Certain drugs, like Opdivo and Keytruda, work by inhibiting PD-1, allowing T-cells to attack malignant cells.

Targeted Mutation Therapy

Drugs that target specific mutations can be prescribed based on biopsies of the cancer you previously had. BRAF and MEK inhibitors are some examples.
